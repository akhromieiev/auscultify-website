What Does Atrial Fibrillation Sound Like?

Recognizing AFib on auscultation · Updated 2026

Atrial fibrillation has one unmistakable signature on auscultation: it is irregularly irregular. The beats arrive with no repeating pattern and no predictable gap, and their intensity varies from beat to beat. Once you've heard it, it's hard to mistake for anything else — the trick is hearing it enough times to trust your ear.

Why AFib sounds the way it does

In AFib the atria quiver instead of contracting in an organized way, so the AV node is bombarded with irregular impulses and lets them through unpredictably. Each ventricular contraction happens after a different filling time, so stroke volume — and therefore the loudness of S1 — changes with every beat. The result: random spacing and random intensity.

How to distinguish it from other irregular rhythms

RhythmWhat you hear
Atrial fibrillationIrregularly irregular — no pattern at all, varying S1 intensity
Premature beats (PACs/PVCs)Regular rhythm with occasional early beats then a pause — a pattern within the irregularity
Second-degree AV block (Wenckebach)Progressive change then a dropped beat — regularly irregular
Sinus arrhythmiaRate speeds up on inspiration, slows on expiration — tied to breathing

A useful bedside clue: in AFib you may notice a pulse deficit — the apical rate is higher than the radial rate, because weak beats don't generate a palpable pulse.

Confirm, don't diagnose, by ear

Auscultation raises your suspicion; an ECG confirms AFib (absent P waves, irregular R–R intervals). But recognizing the rhythm at the bedside is what prompts you to get that ECG — so it's a skill worth having sharp.
