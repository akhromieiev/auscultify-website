S3 vs S4 Heart Sounds: Gallops Explained

A cardiology guide for students and residents · Updated 2026

S3 and S4 are the "extra" heart sounds — the gallops. Both are low-pitched, both are best heard with the bell at the apex, and both trip up students because they sit close to the normal S1 and S2. The trick is timing: one comes right after S2, the other right before S1.

The mnemonics that fix the timing

S3S4
WhenEarly diastole, just after S2Late diastole, just before S1
CadenceS1–S2–S3 = "Ken-TUCK-y"S4–S1–S2 = "TEN-nes-see"
MechanismRapid ventricular filling into a floppy, volume-loaded ventricleAtrium contracting against a stiff, non-compliant ventricle
Typical causeHeart failure, volume overload; normal in kids & young adultsHypertension, LVH, aortic stenosis, ischemia; almost always pathologic in older adults

How to hear them

Use the bell lightly over the apex with the patient in the left lateral decubitus position — this brings the apex to the chest wall and amplifies these low-frequency sounds. Press too hard and the bell becomes a diaphragm, filtering the gallop out entirely. If you hear both S3 and S4 with a fast heart rate, they can merge into a summation gallop.

Why students miss them

S3 and S4 are near the lower limit of hearing, so they're felt as much as heard — a subtle "extra bump" in the rhythm. You will not learn them from a description; you have to hear the Kentucky and Tennessee cadences enough times that your brain flags the deviation from a plain "lub-dub."
